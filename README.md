<p>I’m a software engineer at Microsoft, and I spend most of my time building AI systems for places they’re not supposed to work – air-gapped government networks, on-prem servers with no internet, sovereign clouds where you can’t call anyone else’s API. Chat apps, retrieval over private data, tool-using agents running on models that don’t always cooperate. It’s messy and constrained and I genuinely love it.

The thing I can’t stop thinking about, though, is what’s actually going on inside these models. I’ve been drawn to interpretability since my internship at Novetta in 2020, where I trained GPT-2 to explain its own predictions. These days I run small experiments when I get curious about something – like why reasoning models fight so hard to fix errors in their chain of thought, or whether telling a model to act like a goblin actually changes how it codes. I write them up here because I think more people should poke at these things and share what they find.

I also can’t stop building things for myself. It’s a problem, honestly. I wrote up a philosophy for designing CLIs so AI agents can drive them. I built a pixel-art office for managing coding agents because four terminal tabs was driving me insane. I built an AI coworker that sends me a morning brief, monitors my Teams, and runs 30+ daily workflows. At some point the side projects became the portfolio, which wasn’t the plan, but I’m not complaining.

Before Microsoft I taught data science at Forge for two years. There’s something about watching a student’s face when a concept clicks – I still chase that feeling. I built a 1,200-person early-career engineering community at Microsoft for the same reason. Helping people find their footing early is one of those things I’ll probably never stop doing.

</p>
